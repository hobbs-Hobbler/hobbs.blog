Death Comes Closely Now

Heather Dodds April 6, 2020
 

 

Chances are that, with the COVID-19 pandemic, someone you know, a family member or a friend, will die within this horror. There are already stories of children learning that their parents have died, states away and alone. Spouses are losing their life partner without so much as one last “I Love You” before the insertion of a ventilator. Funerals are currently outlawed. We may run out of coffins. Indeed, death comes closely now.

When it comes to death, people don’t know what to say or do. Additionally, because we are socially isolated now, some of the structures surrounding death will be denied to us. Funeral directors normally help us navigate the first few days of grief; now plans have to be made for ‘when we can get back together’. What do we do when the grief experience is literally and figuratively…remote?

Indeed, it can be hard to find our way through this verse when our very concepts of death and time become tangled and swamped:

Ecclesiastes 3:1,2

1 There is a time for everything,
and a season for every activity under the heavens:

2 a time to be born and a time to die,

I want to offer some words of advice about how to navigate this sea of grief that we are all about to wade unwillingly into. Admittedly, these words work best at the societal level, but they will work at the personal level if you will take them to heart.

Elana Premack Sandler, L.C.S.W., M.P.H, writes about what she calls the Ring Theory with the main idea of Comfort In, Dump Out. Please do read her article at Psychology Today here. It contains some great examples of what to say (or not) and do when death arrives.

 

I would modify her words to “Emote Out, Care In.”

The basic idea here is that whenever tragedy or death occurs, the person closest emotionally to the event (I’ll call them ‘the bereaved’) gets TO HAVE EVERY EMOTION THEY WANT. Yes, this is important. That person can be sad, happy, angry, in denial, relieved, or fearful. That person gets to emote out (dump out) any emotion they want for as long as they want.

The second part is care in. This means that in successive societal circles around the bereaved, each circle does what one can to pour care in towards the bereaved. Each circle has a zone of control where they can execute their powers.

For example, immediately around the bereaved is likely family (spouse, children, siblings). Their care in duties will often be the most personal and direct but also the most hidden and quiet. They may help pick out clothing for the body. They can stay with the bereaved until a few days have passed, making sure that the bereaved can do things like eat, drink, and sleep.

The next outer circle might contain the children’s bosses at their place of employment, who care in by granting as much bereavement leave as possible. Churches often are in this circle too and they can arrange meal delivery or communicate prayer requests. Circles further out are societal structures of updating paperwork or processing a death certificate.

What should NOT happen is mixing the emoting out and caring in.

So DO NOT EMOTE IN.

Examples of emoting in include cruel statements like: “You should be glad he is with our Lord” or “It is blessing that you don’t have to worry about his constant care anymore.” That is pouring an emotion in. No. Never do that. You have no right. NO EMOTION IN towards the center. No level can emote in to a level that is closer to the center.

Also, you can’t expect the bereaved to send care out. This is not the time to ask them to make your favorite cake. It’s just not right.

Now, each person gets to play a role in grieving. Just because a spouse is the center of her circle over the death of her husband doesn’t mean that each child will also be the center of their own circles over the death of their father. So the circles do overlap. The point is to recognize who is closer the rawness of death and cede the emotional center to them at that moment.

I offer this advice as someone for whom death has been a companion. I’ve lost many treasured family members over the past 15 years and learned that grief is not like a wave that you stand up from, recover, brush yourself off, and look ahead. You don’t get over it. You get changed by it. Grief is a like yeast to bread; it works it's way into every part of the loaf. Grief changes you and makes you see perspective.

Grief makes you love deeper, laugh more, and cry harder.

From my experience, I send you hope. Life changes. Sweetness arrives again in birdsong or a child’s laughter. Pain subsides like the tide pulling away. You will begin to fear death less when you realize that we are all together in this journey.

Peace to you.

Psalms 23:4
Even though I walk
through the darkest valley,
I will fear no evil,
for you are with me;
